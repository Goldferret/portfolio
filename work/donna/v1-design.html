Building a system that thinks like I do

Donna — v1.0 — January–May 2026

The idea came out of a frustration I've had for years: I hyperfocus, lose track of time, forget emails, and then can't remember what I did last week. I'd tried productivity tools before and they all shared the same flaw — they expected me to maintain them. A todo app only works if you update it. A calendar only helps if you check it. I don't want to maintain a system. I want a system that maintains itself while I just talk.

The starting point was a question I asked in the design session: "I thought it was that I use you as my interface for talking about goals and work, and then you organize it — I never actually access the database. Is this correct?" The answer was yes, and that became the core principle: Model 1, AI as Interface. Obsidian is the database — a queryable vault with structured metadata on every note — but I never open it directly. Everything goes through conversation.

The first design challenge was figuring out what the system actually needed to do. After a deep interview session covering goals, work style, memory patterns, and how I handle conflict, we built a profile that shaped every subsequent decision. I'm an idea-driven, passive thinker — I don't actively recall, I wait for things to surface. I hyperfocus, which is a strength and a liability simultaneously. I need visible, quantifiable progress or I lose motivation. And I process things through dialogue, not monologue — which is exactly what makes an AI assistant a natural fit rather than just a tool.

The workflow command system came from recognizing that some interactions needed complex, structured prompts to be useful — not ad-hoc conversation. @todos generates a weekly list from tracked commitments, @reflect processes the day's work against what was planned, @review produces a quantified weekly summary. The commands are just shorthand for injecting those prompts, and they store their outputs back into Obsidian behind the scenes.

One design decision that almost went wrong: early on the system was supposed to check in with me proactively after three hours of work. I pushed back when I realized the architecture didn't support unprompted output — there's no background process, no scheduler. The fix was to build elapsed time tracking into the daily notes instead. During any workflow run, I can check how long I've been in a session. It's less automatic, but it fits what the system can actually do, and I learned that working within real constraints beats designing around imaginary ones.

The noun-tagging architecture — flagging people, places, ideas, projects, and organizations as queryable metadata — came from a specific cognitive constraint I mentioned: I remember ideas and places well but struggle with names after time passes. The system needed to support queries like "what did I work on related to ACWC?" or "what happened at UTSA last semester?" not "what did I do with Jeremy in March?" That shaped the entire tagging taxonomy.

The system ran daily from late January through May 2026 — morning briefs, todo generation, weekly reviews, project logging. What worked well: the Obsidian-as-database architecture, the workflow commands, the context continuity across sessions. What needed rethinking: the morning fleet scaling issues, the subagent coordination failures, and the EA behavioral layer — which became v2.0.
